Computers Without Human Intervention
Read Article
M. Ahmed Tayib
02 May 2019
6 MIN READ



When asked about the future, people of 1900’s would imagine according to their own perspective and their own experience which they gained through their past involvement of their life. For example, one of them can easily imagine that there will be flying machines; however, their experience only taught them to stay within their paradigm of understanding and they only imagine a bicycle with hot-air balloons. That is, whatever their imaginary capacity is, they cannot think outside the box unless the box would kink a little. In fact, this situation holds for our time too. When asked about the upcoming aspects of computers, individuals literally blow their minds. The terror of AI, brutal robots, the operating systems planning world domination etc. Nevertheless, we must not leap into thousands of meters forward to see our near future.

The retroperspective notion may help us to comprehend this problem. We cannot leap outside of our box neither we can think outside of it; however, when we try to do so we take our past actions into account. Thus, we barely imagine going directly to moon with a submarine since it was the most innovative thing before and during our time. Just like moon voyager submarine, the terminator robots are coming to our minds since our experience with technology is so young and brittle.

Aside from cheesy Hollywood films, the human-computer-interaction (HCI) is a significant study subject. From mere ease of use designs to complex Turing Tests and indecisiveness situations the human interaction with computers are rich in terms of content. Said study was emerged due to need of user experience improvements in computers. The multidisciplinary nature of the field focused generally on design of computers and the interaction of users with it. However, the design of computers with HCI in mind started to bend the box, which we tried to think outside of it.

Autonomic Computing

Just like human neural system computers also have a core processing unit(s). However, comparing human biology and computer architecture with each other would be a bad mistake since although same analogy is nice have around for explaining simple things, exact association would be total trouble. Those analogies were done billions of times for both praising human brain over computers and vice versa. However, they are two different things that do not need exact relation or comparison. The simplest input-output systems are much better than human brains in repetitive task whereas most complex supercomputers cannot compose a symphony. Nevertheless, they do not need to!

What a human brain cannot do is that it cannot teach itself on the things that it didn’t even heard. However, latest computing systems proved that they can learn to control further actions of their existence. The self-management of computers is not a thing of far future; in fact, computers now keep an eye on their functioning and repeatedly operate maintenance task to keep themselves upgraded. Although, it was an indecisive move in first years of IT, now computers can operate until infinity! Other promising aspect of autonomic computers is that they are self-controlling. That is, they can configure themselves in different situations. Those dynamic adjustments mostly occur automatically and numerous times in incremental times.

The best part of autonomic computing is that they have self-learning aspect that is they can learn from their past, from other’s past, from a sample data, and from only factoring out irrelevances. Thus, they are like human neural system which can evolve through tries and errors. Complex speaking and listening tasks are expected to be performed by AI systems thanks to their learning capacities in near future.

Therefore, apart from the discussion of who is smarter, the real debate must be on when computers will not need humans. In fact, some part of them is promisingly need less human involvement; however, the current mindset will probably hit the wall in near future. The problem of ongoing computer design is that philosophical aspect of the problems are outside of the box; however, the solutions are being looked inside. Once proposed by famous mathematician Gödel, Gödel’s Incompleteness Theorem suggests that the outcomes within closed logical systems are limited and deriving conclusion from them may or may not be correct; thus, programming languages being a “closed logical system”, cannot solve their own problems if the designs are kept stationary. Unfortunately, the mindset of computer designers is still in retrospective, they cannot totally think outside of the box since the structures are still mere replicates of human brain and neural system. However, autonomic computers proved that even a little structural break can change paradigm on IT. Hence, instead of replicating human brain, computer structures must follow a different road since with outdated mindsets we can only solve the past’s problems, not today’s.

If you are interested in that subject, I strongly suggest you to read Rick Robinson’s internet article 11 reasons computers can’t understand or solve our problems without human judgement.
