Cluster Analysis vs Factor Analysis
Read Article
Serkan Oğuz
29 May 2019
4 MIN READ



All about cluster analysis and multifactored analysis were mentioned in our blog in meticulous detail. However, a comprehensive comparison had not been made until now. The exact reason of this comparison is to show users that both methods have their ups and downs. However, selection is on the user’s verdict in which personal taste and the outcome of choice will be important. In fact, both methods have specific strength to one another while dependent and independent variables are at stake. While the sole objective of methods seems to differentiate, the outcome more or less the same between cluster analysis and multifactored (or factored analysis). Nevertheless, there is an important point to mention that factor analysis and multifactored analysis are different things that can be used interchangeably exclusive to this matter where we discuss statistical storytelling.

A Little Glimpse of Cluster Analysis

As we mentioned earlier, cluster data groups the observations in an order that sample points indicate similarities of chosen notion. Likewise, called as segmentation or taxonomy analysis, cluster analysis does not differentiate the dependent and independent variables. Thus, the main objective of cluster analysis is to find the heterogeneity in the data; however, some other objectives such as identification, simplification, hypothesis testing, and correlation identification can be listed.

Although cluster analysis goes hand in hand with factor analysis, there are some strong assumptions to make before starting to work with a cluster analysis such as:

  • Sample is thought to be a representative of the population
  • There is no correlation between variables.

Those strong (sometimes unrealistic) assumptions caused some criticism among statisticians that cluster analysis being too descriptive and theoretical, and there are some scholars suggesting that this method produce cluster even if there is no such grouping, and the most of the opposers defend the idea that cluster analysis cannot be widely used as the chosen variables may be insignificant no matter what the grouping says.

A Piece of Factor Analysis

As we mentioned in previous post about factor analysis , it is more of an explorative analysis where data is reduced into pieces for simplification and easy observation of trends in data. In fact, there are types of factor analysis that can be used in different fields of research. One type is confirmatory where researcher tests a specific hypothesis in formed structed, while other types is explanatory where researcher has no hypothesis in mind. The sole objectives of the said analysis are to identify fundamental factors and choosing a representative in models to characterize the group. However, there are some strong assumptions again to look when conducting a factor analysis such as:

  • Model must be a linear one
  • There must be a strong correlation among variables that can be extended to other variables too.

Above strong assumptions unfortunately limit the usage of factor analysis; however, the variety of types and the feasibility of the outcomes make the factor analysis a strong choice.

Verdict

When it comes to choose a method for data analysis, cluster analysis and factor analysis come to the scene as similar methods. However, they are totally different in terms of their objectives, applications and the solution to data models. Thus, a pre-defined hypothesis and a roadmap of needs are musts for the reliability of the analysis. Therefore, due to their applicational differences and objectives, a factor analysis more or less provides a better aspect to researcher as factor inputs can be identified and a respective analysis can be conducted.

Therefore, when it comes to simplify a complex model, identify the group features, predict the future outcomes, and find underlying factors factor analysis is the best. As we had mentioned in our previous posts, multifactored analysis is even better alternative to come up with a solution to complex problems of data sets.
