Why Startups Fail
Read Article
Serkan Oğuz
24 April 2019
5 MIN READ



Whether it’s million-dollar company or a newly found garage-establishment, no matter the size or the scope of the enterprise, some of survive while others gradually fall back in history. Hundreds of hours of researches, preparations, and indefinite brain-storming would not guarantee one’s survival in the jungle of business world. Moreover, doing something perfectly while doing another thing not-so-perfectly almost always result in failures. The nature of startups in 21st century mostly consists of data science, app generation, new media, and so on. Those areas due to their innovative and fresh aspects are less prone to failures, at least this is the intuition. However, things are not always go as planned. Rather than scapegoating the luck factor, some unfortunate miscalculations and mispredictions are the real cause of failure of startups.

  • Miscalculating Market Fit: whatever business you are starting, a good market-research is a must. However, an undeniable part of startups of are going out of business because they had calculated or predicted the market need of their product/service totally wrong. Although there isn’t any one correct way of starting a good market need analysis, a good analysis starts with an intuition where business owners must ask themselves whether they are presenting something customers really need.
  • Financial Issues: This is the most obvious one. Many of the enterprises go out of business when they across a financial drawback even in their first time. The misprediction of short-run and long-run economic models are main causes; nonetheless, the financial issues are two-faceted: low starting funds are deadly due their vulnerability to shortcomings where to much funds are as much as deadly due to their danger of underestimation of allocation.
  • Wrong Crew: This is as well intuitive, o good team can make a company billion-dollar worth; however, a wrong one can’t event move a step forward. A motived, diversely-skilled organized group can make a huge difference when it comes to last-minute decisions and long-run achievements.
  • Bad Marketing: Although what presented by startup is a smooth, quality-product, the target audience must see or at least hear it to buy. Without correct anticipation of the target audience and correct ways of reaching that audience, said product is destined to fail as well as the company which offers it.
  • High Competition in Market: Competition is a nice thing from the perspective of customer since the quality goes up, prices go down, and customer feel like s/he is the center of the world. However, from enterprise side, an overdose competition is deadly. Startups’ nature does not compel a high dosage of competition since their existence is depending on other startups to (LINK), a little bit more would be; unfortunately, deadly.
  • Not considering Customers: This no surprise as well as not specific to startups only. Not obtaining customer feedback and acting accordingly, is a mistake done widely. Instead of upgrading and tweaking the product according to customer needs, owners take their own way blindfolded which causes unescapable failure.
  • Bad Timing: This is not a doctrinal aspect of this essay since the timing concept is highly related with luck. However, a good prediction analysis can outrun the distorted effect of luck. Whether it is related with luck or another thing, timing of an investment highly correlated with its success as well as its long-run survivability.
  • Wrong Prediction of Prices: A good product at a wrong price is as bad as a poor product. Even though a good market fitness is observed about the product, if the customer behavior is wrongly anticipated, the market reacts as it sees a poorly designed product. In fact, this is not wrong since a products success strongly correlates with its pricing strategy.
  • Problematic Products: This also is not surprise for a bad product will not find its place in the market no matter how hard the enterprises tried. An unfinished service, poorly designed products, buggy program and so on will always show themselves in the market for a short time; however, for a long-sustained success the offered unit must consistent if not flawless.

The list can go up to hundredth point, however, there is no need to scale what is obvious. A good prediction and sound strategy (a bit of luck also) is all an enterprise need. If the prediction tools and strategy modelling are consistent, a startup would find its way up to million-dollar league.
